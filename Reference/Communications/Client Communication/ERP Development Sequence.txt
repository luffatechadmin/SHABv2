ERP Development Sequence
1. Supply Chain / Procurement (START HERE)

This is the entry point of all materials and costs.

Why first:

Raw materials (chemicals, packaging, ingredients) start here
Supplier data feeds costing, inventory, and production
Mistakes here cascade everywhere

Meet & define:

Supplier onboarding & qualification (important for cosmetics compliance)
Purchase workflows (MOQ, lead time, pricing tiers)
Material specifications (INCI names, batch traceability)
Regulatory documents (COA, MSDS)
2. Inventory / Warehouse Management

After procurement, materials land here.

Why second:

Inventory is the bridge between purchasing and production
Cosmetics require strict batch/lot tracking & expiry control

Meet & define:

Batch/lot tracking (critical for GMP compliance)
Expiry & shelf-life management
Storage conditions (temperature, segregation)
Stock movements (receiving, transfer, adjustment)
3. Manufacturing / Production (Core of OEM business)

This is where value is created.

Why third:

Depends on accurate materials & inventory data
Drives product output, costing, and planning

Meet & define:

Bill of Materials (BOM) for each formula
Work orders & production planning
Batch production records (BPR)
Yield, wastage, and rework tracking
Filling, labeling, packaging processes

👉 For cosmetics OEM, also include:

Formula versioning (R&D updates)
Stability tracking
4. Quality Control (QC/QA)

In cosmetics, this is not optional—it's central.

Why here:

QC sits tightly with manufacturing but depends on production data
Needed for compliance (GMP, ISO, NPRA in Malaysia)

Meet & define:

Incoming material QC
In-process QC checks
Finished goods testing
Non-conformance & CAPA
Release/hold/reject workflows
5. Sales / Customer Order Management

Now connect production to revenue.

Why after production:

You need to know what you can produce before committing to clients
OEM often deals with custom formulations and MOQs

Meet & define:

Customer onboarding (brand owners)
Quotation & pricing models
Sales orders linked to production
Custom formulation requests
Contract manufacturing terms
6. Finance / Accounting

This consolidates everything into money.

Why later:

Finance depends on accurate upstream data
Otherwise you’ll rebuild it multiple times

Meet & define:

Costing (raw materials + production + overhead)
Accounts payable (suppliers)
Accounts receivable (clients)
Profitability by product / client
Inventory valuation
7. HR / Workforce Management (LAST)

Support function—not core transaction driver.

Why last:

Less dependency on operational flow
Easier to integrate later

Meet & define:

Workforce scheduling (production shifts)
Payroll
Training records (important for GMP compliance)
Performance tracking
🔄 Visual Flow (Think Like This)

Suppliers → Inventory → Production → Quality → Sales → Finance → HR

That’s the data flow of reality, not just departments.

⚠️ Important Strategic Advice
1. Don’t Build Department Silos

Even if you meet separately, always map:

Inputs they receive
Outputs they produce
Who they depend on
2. Start With a “Master Data Workshop”

Before all departments (or right after procurement):

Define:

Product codes
Raw material codes
Units of measure
Batch/lot structure

👉 If this is wrong → entire ERP fails later

3. Cosmetics Industry-Specific Must-Haves

Make sure your ERP supports:

Batch traceability (forward & backward)
Expiry tracking
Regulatory documentation (NPRA / ASEAN)
Formula confidentiality (client IP protection)
4. Build in Phases (Recommended)

Instead of one big launch:

Phase 1: Procurement + Inventory
Phase 2: Manufacturing + QC
Phase 3: Sales + Finance
Phase 4: HR + advanced analytics
